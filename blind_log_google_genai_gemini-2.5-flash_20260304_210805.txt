### How the Checksum Word is Determined

1.  **Entropy:** The first 11 words (for a 12-word phrase) represent 128 bits of entropy.
2.  **Hashing:** A cryptographic hash function (SHA256) is applied to this 128-bit entropy.
3.  **Checksum Bits:** The first few bits of the resulting hash are taken as the checksum. For a 12-word phrase (128 bits of entropy), the checksum is 4 bits long.
4.  **Combining:** These 4 checksum bits are appended to the original 128 bits of entropy, making a total of 132 bits.
5.  **Word Mapping:** These 132 bits are then divided into 12 groups of 11 bits each. Each 11-bit group corresponds to an index in the 2048-word BIP-39 wordlist, giving you the 12 words of the phrase.

So, the last word isn't *just* a checksum of the first 11 words; it's derived from the entropy that *includes* the checksum bits. More accurately, the *entire* 132-bit sequence (128 entropy + 4 checksum) is what gets mapped to the 12 words. The checksum ensures that the *combination* of all 12 words is valid.
